\section*{Part 4: Methods and Results}

\subsection*{Scope and research questions}
We study PJM nodal day-ahead LMP (primary price signal) with NOAA Integrated Surface Database (ISD) weather aligned to each pricing node. \textbf{RQ1} asks whether the strength of the weather--grid \emph{association} (correlation) has shifted or differs between nodes classified as data-center (DC)--likely versus not. \textbf{RQ2} asks whether \emph{weather-only} predictive models exhibit systematically different error in DC-likely versus other nodes. All tables and figures below are derived from analysis exports or model metrics JSON---not ad hoc screenshots of spreadsheets.

\subsection*{Data acquisition and alignment}
\textbf{Energy.} Day-ahead full-system LMP is read from PJM parquet archives (hourly \texttt{total\_lmp\_da} by \texttt{pnode\_id} and timestamp); load-related rows use forecast load fields where included in the training script. Timestamps are parsed and aligned to UTC.

\textbf{Weather.} For each node, ISD hourly CSVs are read by station and year; fields are converted to physical units (temperature and dew point from tenths \si{\celsius}, sea-level pressure from tenths hPa, wind from tenths \si{\meter\per\second}, precipitation from tenths mm). Sentinel codes are treated as missing. Cooling and heating degree hours (CDH, HDH) use base \SI{18}{\celsius}; relative humidity uses a Magnus-form estimate from temperature and dew point.

\textbf{Merge.} For each node--year, energy and weather series are merged on time using \texttt{pandas.merge\_asof} in UTC with a \SI{30}{minute} tolerance so each LMP hour matches the nearest weather observation; unmatched hours are excluded from correlation and feature matrices.

\subsection*{Data-center labeling (for bucket comparisons)}
Nodes are scored in \texttt{scripts/export\_pjm\_metadata.py} using \texttt{scripts/dc\_region\_scoring.py} (geographic proximity to known DC-heavy footprints, optional attributes, confidence). Each node receives \texttt{classificationLabel} and \texttt{isDataCenterHeavy}; correlation and model rows carry \texttt{dc} vs.\ \texttt{nonDc} buckets consistent with that export. Reproducibility requires re-running the metadata export before rebuilding correlations or models when labels change.

\subsection*{RQ1: correlation construction}
For each node, calendar year, weather variable (e.g.\ CDH, HDH, temperature, RH, wind, pressure, precipitation), and target (\texttt{lmp} and/or \texttt{load}), we compute Pearson correlation between aligned weather and target series. Period tags follow the export script: \texttt{earlyAI} corresponds to \num{2021} rows and \texttt{recentAI} to \num{2022}--\num{2025}. The app-facing file \texttt{data/exports/correlations\_by\_region\_period.json} stores each coefficient with \texttt{nodeId}, \texttt{regionId}, \texttt{year}, \texttt{isDataCenterHeavyBucket}, and \texttt{target}.

\subsection*{RQ2: predictive models and metrics}
\textbf{Features.} Weather-based inputs (e.g.\ \texttt{temp\_c}, \texttt{dew\_c}, \texttt{rh\_pct}, \texttt{wind\_ms}, \texttt{slp\_hpa}, \texttt{precip\_mm}, \texttt{cdh}, \texttt{hdh}) plus simple calendar indicators as implemented in \texttt{scripts/train\_rq2\_models.py}.

\textbf{Models.} Six scikit-learn pipelines: LinearRegression, Ridge ($\alpha{=}1$), Lasso ($\alpha{=}0.001$, high iteration cap), RandomForest (\num{90} trees), GradientBoosting, ExtraTrees (\num{100} trees). Continuous inputs use median imputation; linear pipelines add \texttt{StandardScaler}.

\textbf{Evaluation.} Chronological train/test split with holdout starting at \texttt{--test-start-year} (default \num{2024}) so test metrics reflect later calendar years. We record RMSE, MAE, $R^2$, and weather-only $\mathrm{nRMSE}=\mathrm{RMSE}/|\bar{y}|$ on the test split when $\bar{y}$ is available. Rows are tagged by node, period, target, model name, and DC bucket. Output: \texttt{data/exports/model\_performance.json}.

\subsection*{Between-group statistical summaries}
\texttt{scripts/build\_hypothesis\_exports.py} reads \texttt{nodes.json}, correlations, and model metrics. For RQ1 it aggregates node-level mean absolute correlation per DC bucket; for RQ2 it uses weather-only rows and prefers $n$RMSE when present. It computes bootstrap \num{95}\% CIs (\num{5000} resamples, fixed seed) for DC minus non-DC mean differences when group sizes exceed configured gates, and two-sided permutation $p$-values (\num{3000} shuffles) when eligible; otherwise rows are marked descriptive-only.

\subsection*{Figure generation (for the report)}
Trend panels (Fig.~\ref{fig:rq1time}) are produced by \path{docs/generate_report_figures.py} from the correlation export (LMP target). Distribution and mean-difference panels (Figs.~\ref{fig:rq1cross}--\ref{fig:rq2both}) are produced by \path{docs/generate_rq_research_figures.py}, which reads the same exports plus \texttt{hypothesis\_tests.json}. \subsubsection*{Significance for RQ1}
Taken together, Figs.~\ref{fig:rq1time}--\ref{fig:rq1cross} and Table~\ref{tab:hyp} suggest that \textbf{DC-likely PJM nodes are not clearly separated from other nodes by a stronger or weaker weather--price association in the aggregate}: yearly tracks overlap, within-bucket spread is large, and the formal DC minus non-DC contrast on mean node-level mean $|r|$ is negligible in magnitude (means both $\approx\num{0.135}$), with a \num{95}\% CI spanning zero ($[-0.010,\,0.011]$) and $p=\num{0.945}$.
Interpreting RQ1 in substantive terms, we do \emph{not} see evidence here that ``data-center-heavy context'' coincides with a distinct \emph{population mean} correlation profile relative to non-DC nodes under our labeling and time split; weather remains a measurable correlate for both groups, and time variation appears shared rather than uniquely divergent for DC-likely nodes.

\subsubsection*{Significance for RQ2}
Figure~\ref{fig:rq2both} and Table~\ref{tab:hyp} suggest that \textbf{DC-likely nodes are not convincingly ``harder to predict'' with weather-only models at the level of the population mean error in this run}: distributions overlap heavily, and the DC minus non-DC mean $n$RMSE contrast is small ($\approx\num{0.465}$ vs.\ $\approx\num{0.457}$, $\approx\num{1.6}\,\%$ relative) with a CI that includes zero ($[-0.045,\,0.058]$) and $p=\num{0.791}$.
Substantively, we should not conclude that data-center-adjacent pricing nodes are systematically less weather-explainable by this metric; any slight descriptive gap is dominated by node-to-node and model-configuration variability.
